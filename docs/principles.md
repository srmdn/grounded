# Principles

Grounded is built on a small set of principles that apply across domains. They are not startup advice. They are product discipline.

## Clarity before code

Writing code is cheap. Understanding what to build is not.

Before writing anything, clarify:
- who this is for
- what painful problem it solves
- what the smallest useful version is

If those three lines are vague, the work is not ready.

## Clarity before scale

Scaling a confused product makes confusion bigger, not better. Most projects need sharper focus, not broader reach. A clear, small product is more valuable than a confused, growing one.

## Constraints are part of the design

Constraints are not obstacles to overcome. They are the shape of the solution.

Real constraints include:
- limited budget
- limited time
- limited attention
- limited energy
- limited knowledge today

Great products emerge from working inside constraints, not ignoring them.

## Product pressure over builder ego

Build for the user's need, not the builder's identity. Ship what is useful, not what is impressive. The smallest useful version beats the most elegant architecture.

## Small milestones over grand plans

Big plans create fake progress. Small milestones create real evidence.

A milestone is:
- small enough to finish in days, not months
- concrete enough to validate
- useful even if nothing else follows

## Validation over decoration

Real signal beats polished presentation. Before adding more features, more design, or more complexity, validate what is already built.

Validation means:
- your own repeated use
- a real user trying it
- data that confirms or rejects an assumption

## Next sane step

When stuck between too many options, ask:

> What is the next one thing that moves this forward, is small enough to finish, and will give real signal?

Not the perfect next step. The sane one.

## Anti fake productivity

Movement is not progress. Output is not evidence.

Fake productivity includes:
- reorganizing files instead of shipping
- researching tools instead of building
- polishing before validating
- adding features nobody asked for
- planning endlessly instead of executing

Real progress is small, validated, and visible.
